Senior writer Tom Junod on Muhammad Ali’s lasting imprint

In early February, I visited a business in Louisville called Muldoon Memorials, which has been making gravestones since the 1850s and had made Muhammad Ali’s. I met Cullen McBroom, who told me that he was the one who had designed Ali’s gravestone. He says he had kept the design on his desk for years with the name left off so that nobody would know that Ali was contemplating his own mortality. ESPN director of sports analytics Ben Alamar on the limits of fame

At first, I was surprised to see that no MLB or NHL players made our World Fame 100 list. But the reasoning for their absence is simple: Baseball and hockey players are not international household names. As a result, these players lack global endorsement deals and the media coverage that would put them on the fame map. Senior writer Kurt Streeter on Roger Federer’s tableside manner

During my sit-down interview with Federer in Seattle, a waitress kept coming up to ask if there was anything we needed. Water? Food? Were we comfortable? Federer kept telling her “Thank you” very sincerely, each time looking straight into her eyes. He gave off no sense of privilege, no sense of dismissiveness. I took these moments as significant—I’ve seen many famous people operate quite differently, but Federer talked about how he views politeness as a grounding mechanism, a deeply ingrained part of his value system, his code.
