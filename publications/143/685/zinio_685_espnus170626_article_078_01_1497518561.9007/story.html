2008, 2010 CHAMPION

It’s been a long time since Rafael Nadal has excelled at Wimbledon. A wrist injury forced him to withdraw from the tournament last year, and in his four previous appearances at the All England Lawn Tennis & Croquet Club Limited, Nadal has only once advanced to the round of 16. But 2017 has been a renaissance year for the Spanish southpaw, beginning with a run to the final of the Australian Open. In his early days, his game appeared ill-equipped for grass, but Nadal has proved himself a man for all surfaces, taking the Wimbledon title twice (’08, ’10) and reaching the final three times (’06, ’07, ’11).

2016 FINALIST

From his early days as a tennis player, Raonic had his sights set on Wimbledon. After all, Raonic’s hero was seven-time Wimbledon champion Pete Sampras. And like Sampras, Raonic has built his game on aggression, confidence and striking boldly on big points. At 6-5, Raonic anchors his game with a smooth, powerful serve that he can pinpoint anywhere. Last year at Wimbledon, Raonic beat Roger Federer in the semis before losing to Andy Murray in the final. Still just 26 years old, Raonic will likely be a factor at Wimbledon for many years to come.

2016 THIRD-ROUNDER

Three years ago, American Jack Sock tasted victory at Wimbledon, winning the doubles title with Vasek Pospisil in a five-set thriller over the Bryan brothers. The power Sock displayed on that run—most of all, his blistering serve and bazooka-like forehand, a shot often struck at a staggering 3,300-plus rpm—revealed his considerable arsenal and zest for playing high-stakes matches in places like Wimbledon’s Centre Court. Having won only three matches in his previous three Wimbledons, Sock should be poised to make his way into Wimbledon’s second week.

2014-2016 THIRD-ROUNDER

You might well remember American John Isner as the victor in the longest match in tennis history—a 2010 Wimbledon first-round battle that lasted just over 11 hours, won by Isner over Frenchman Nicolas Mahut 70-68 in the fifth. And you’d think that a man with as big a serve as Isner would have what it took for a good run at Wimbledon. But oddly enough, the 32-year-old’s career Wimbledon record is 9-8—his best efforts three straight trips to the third round in ’14, ’15 and ’16.

2014 QUARTERFINALIST

Electric, athletic, confident—Australian Nick Kyrgios has all the attributes that add up to success at Wimbledon. At his best, Kyrgios exemplifies the old saying that fortune favors the bold. This surfaced most vividly three years ago in his first Wimbledon. Kyrgios slashed his way to the quarters, along the way taking out such formidable opponents as Richard Gasquet and Rafael Nadal. His biggest weapons are his lightning-bolt serve, vicious forehand and adventurous spirit no matter where he is on the court.
