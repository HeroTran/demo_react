Workers in UK need support

June 1, 2017, LONDON, England—Nearly half of British public sector workers reported taking sick days for mental health reasons, versus a third of workers in the private sector, the Independent reports. However, private sector workers were more likely to say they felt supported after disclosing mental health problems.

 

Philippines' mental health bill

May 2, 2017, MANILA, Philippines—The Senate here unanimously approved a bill to establish a national mental health policy. The legislation would integrate behavioral health care into the public health system and require the government to provide mental health services at the community level and in public hospitals.

 

Hong Kong lacks specialists

April 27, 2017, HONG KONG, China—Hong Kong faces a severe shortage of psychiatrists in public hospitals and wait times to see a mental health expert range from nine months to three years. A government report noted increased demand for services, especially among children, the Hong Kong Free Press reports.

 

Guyana targets suicide rate

April 15, 2017, GEORGETOWN, Guyana—This South American country, which has the world’s highest suicide rate, announced plans for a national campaign tackling mental health issues. Experts cite deep poverty, alcohol abuse, easy access to deadly pesticides, and limited access to psychiatrists for the high rate of self-inflicted deaths.

 

MLB player Jimmy Piersall was a pioneer in chronicling his bipolar disorder

June 4, 2017, BOSTON, MA—Former pro baseball player Jim Piersall, who opened up about his struggles with bipolar disorder in his book Fear Strikes Out, has died. The 1955 memoir was later made into a movie starring Anthony Perkins and Karl Malden.

Known for his on-field antics, Piersall returned to the majors after being admitted to a psychiatric facility. He went public about his struggles with manic depression, now known as bipolar disorder, to combat society’s stereotypes of mental disorders, the Associated Press reports.

After his playing days, Piersall became a broadcaster. He was inducted into the Red Sox Hall of Fame in 2010.

 

Proposed bill directs US agency to tailor outreach for Asian-Americans

June 1, 2017, WASHINGTON, DC —A bill introduced in the U.S. House of Representatives aims to reduce stubborn stigma that keeps Asian-Americans and Pacific Islanders suffering in silence.

The bill would mandate the Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration to reach out to those communities to raise awareness about behavioral health. According to the American Psychological Association, those population groups are three times less likely to seek mental health care than Americans of European descent, NBC News reports.

Rep. Judy Chu of California, who introduced the bill, said language barriers and cultural beliefs contribute to lower rates of help-seeking. The “model minority” myth, which wrongly imagines that Asian-Americans don’t have the same problems as other groups, only makes it worse, she said.

 

Coalition looks at immigrants’ issues

May 26, 2017, CHICAGO, IL—The Coalition for Immigrant Mental Health hosted its first summit to discuss psychological issues faced by individuals living in the United States without legal permission.

Community leaders, representatives from public health agencies, elected officials and experts from Chicago-area universities touched on issues like chronic stress related to uncertain immigration status and the federal government’s pressure on “sanctuary cities.”

U.S. Sen. Dick Durbin talked about “emotional turmoil” due to increased anti-immigration sentiment in the wake of last year’s presidential election.

 

New Colorado law ends jailing during mental health holds

May 18, 2017, DENVER, CO—Gov. John Hickenlooper signed into law new legislation that will ban putting people in jail when they are picked up on mental health holds, the Denver Post reports.

Colorado had been one of just six states that permitted placing people behind bars when they are having mental health episodes or are suicidal, but have not committed any crime.

The new legislation also increases funding for walk-in mental health centers and a network of crisis-response teams, as well as transportation to treatment in rural areas.

 

Exhibit at Boston’s Museum of Science aims to change attitudes about living with bipolar

May 27, 2017, BOSTON, MA—The Museum of Science in this New England capital opened an exhibit intended to dispel misconceptions about brain-based disorders, the Newburyport News reports.

Titled “Many Faces of Our Mental Health,” the exhibit features artworks inspired by current research. One sculpture represents the full human DNA sequence. A display of 99 photographs challenges viewers to tell who has bipolar disorder, who has schizophrenia, and who is a family member with no diagnosis.

“This is not to say [a serious mental illness] goes away, and not to say it’s not an enormous challenge, but success is out there and … I wanted to show the hope,” said Lynda Michaud Cutrell, who pulled the exhibit together with assistance from the National Alliance on Mental Illness (NAMI) Cape Ann.

 

Sunovion Hosts Mental Health Influencer Summit

May 21, 2017, SAN DIEGO, CA—Sunovion Pharmaceuticals Inc. sponsored their 2nd Annual Mental Health Influencer Summit in May. Under the theme of courage and commitment, the event united top online influencers to discuss ways to advance mental health together. Keynote speaker Jennifer Marshall, co-founder of This Is My Brave, tied the mission of the grassroots organization to the day’s objective and showcased how the power of storytelling can build a sense of community, helping people receive the treatment and support they need to live well. Fellow keynote Robert Goldman, MD, head of Global Clinical Research & Medical Affairs at Sunovion, underscored the importance of continued mental health research, revealing how new innovations can further lead to hope for the future.

 

Mounties in Alberta required to take resiliency training

April 30, 2017, SPRUCE GROVE, AB—The Royal Canadian Mounted Police (RCMP) division in Alberta is using a program called the Road to Mental Readiness (R2MR) in an effort to prevent post-traumatic stress disorder and other mental health issues, the Spruce Grove Examiner reports.

The program, which is mandatory in the RCMP’s K Division, provides information on the short-term and long-term effects of trauma, teaches self-management strategies and resiliency skills, and directs those in need to additional resources.

It’s also an effort to challenge stigma in what can be a very stressful work environment. Const. Shelley Nasheim said officers are often hesitant to admit they need help out of fear they’ll be viewed as weak, unable to do their work, or “faking it.” The R2MR workshop “gives us permission to come forward and deal with it, because not dealing with it is causing nothing but harm,” Nasheim said.
