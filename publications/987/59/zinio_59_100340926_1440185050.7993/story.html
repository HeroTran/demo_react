BY JON L. JACOBI

IF YOU WANT to work incognito at the coffee shop or blend discreetly into the boardroom, Acer’s squarish, matte-black TravelMate Pro (go.pcworld.com/acertravelmate) is the laptop to use. It doesn’t flip, fold, detach, or do any other fancy tricks. But the TravelMate Pro is light, rugged, secure, and fast.

The TMP645-MG-9419 version of the TravelMate Pro that Acer sent us came outfitted with an Intel Core i7-4500u processor, 8GB of DDR3/1600 memory, a discrete AMD Radeon HD 8750M graphics processor, and a 256GB Toshiba solid-state drive.

The laptop’s quick and smooth performance earned the Acer a score of 68 in our Notebook WorldBench 9 test suite. (Note that PCWorld’s WorldBench scale has changed, and the new reference point is a much faster laptop than was used to produce WorldBench 8 and WorldBench 8.1 scores.)

And thanks to its discrete GPU, the TravelMate Pro even managed to deliver playable frame rates with games such as BioShock Infinite and Grid 2 (albeit at a resolution of 1024 by 768 pixels). Still, that’s something no laptop with integrated graphics can accomplish. Video playing at the display’s native resolution of 1920 by 1080 pixels was butter-smooth, but movie soundtracks sounded blah.

If the TravelMate’s appearance isn’t particularly compelling, its carbon-fiber chassis and all-metal hinges are. This laptop is very sturdy, though the bezel surrounding the 14-inch, IPS display flexes quite a bit.

There’s at least one design oddity that will irk an IT department: The screws securing the access plate covering the hard drive and memory slots are of two different gauges. Not expecting this, I inadvertently stripped one of the smaller screws. Who wants to change screwdrivers twice every time they open an access panel? Certainly not anyone charged with managing a fleet of notebooks.

A gigabit ethernet port stands out among ports and connectivity options, which are mostly what you’d expect to find on a notebook in this class: three USB 3.0 ports, VGA and HDMI video outputs, and separate mic and headphone jacks. Wireless functions include Bluetooth 4, but the Wi-Fi adapter tops out at 802.11n—we’d be happier with 802.11ac. PCWorld favors DisplayPort connectors over HDMI, but you’ll need to buy Acer’s $150 ProDock to get that. A cheap 0.9-megapixel webcam is mounted in the display’s bezel, with a single integrated mic.

The TravelMate Pro’s input ergonomics are very sound. The touchpad is silky, and the backlit, island-style keyboard has full-size keys with decent tactile feedback. The notebook is also outfitted with a biometric fingerprint reader and a docking port on its underbelly.

PCWorld favors DisplayPort connectors over HDMI, but you’ll need to buy Acer’s $150 ProDock to get that.

Acer promises that TravelMate Pro models will remain essentially the same for 12 to 18 months from introduction, and that replacement parts will be available for at least three years.

The unit comes with a two-year mail-in/carry-in warranty, which can be extended one additional year. On-site and other service options are also available at extra cost. This information is a bit difficult to find, as Acer hides it from anyone whose laptop is not registered.

At $1300, this TravelMate Pro is quite a nice piece of hardware, and it’s significantly less expensive than much of its business-oriented competition. But the lack of a five-year warranty, the shortish coverage, and niggling details like different-gauge screws will irk the corporate element of its intended audience. The favorable price-to-performance ratio makes it a go for independent business users, but for corporate fleets? Probably not.

Acer TravelMate Pro (Model P645-MG-9419)

PROS:

• Very good performance

• Long battery life

• Docking station option

CONS:

• Aesthetically dull

• Bottom access plate uses two gauges of screws

• Optional warranties limited to just three years

BOTTOM LINE:

For solo business users, this laptop offers a nice blend of modern features and solid performance. It’s not stylish, but its input ergonomics are very good.
