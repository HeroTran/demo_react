CLOUD STORAGE SERVICES SUCH AS DROPBOX, GOOGLE DRIVE, AND SUGARSYNC ARE CONVENIENT, EFFICIENTÆ¬AND NOTORIOUSLY INSECURE.

Files are rarely encrypted, data transfer is typically not protected, and companies are usually able to access your files (even if they state that they won’t, they could be legally compelled to do so).

Documents such as business plans or other sensitive files (say, a copy of your birth certificate) should be protected. You can use a special, extra-secure provider such as Wuala, Tresorit, or McAfee Personal Locker, or you can encrypt files yourself before uploading them to larger storage services, such as Dropbox.

WUALA

PRICE: 5GB free; plans start at 20GB for $4 a month

PLATFORMS: Windows, Linux, iOS, Android

Wuala (www.wuala.com) is a secure cloud storage service offered by storage company LaCie. This service differs from mainstream cloud storage providers in two ways:

Client-side encryption of files: It encrypts all of your files locally on your device before sending them to the cloud, which ensures that, even with a nonencrypted transfer, no readable data will leak out. This process is even more secure than a secure transfer, because nobody but you ever has access to your data.

Zero-knowledge password policy: Only you know your password, and therefore only you can access your account. Wuala’s employees cannot see your password, nor can they see anything about your data other than how many files you have and how much storage space the files take up. So even if the government came knocking on Wuala’s door and asked the company to turn over your files, it simply wouldn’t be able to do so. Nor will you be able to get to your stuff if you forget your password, however, so safeguard it carefully.

Security aside, Wuala operates like the cloud storage services you’re used to. Simply download Wuala’s application, and the service will install a special sync folder on your device, where you can drag and drop files to store both locally and in the cloud. Wuala also offers backup and versioning, which means you’ll be able to access previous versions of files or restore files should you accidentally delete them. Like other cloud storage providers, Wuala offers 5GB of storage for free. Pricing plans start at 20GB for $4 per month.

Only you know your password, so only you can access your account: Wuala’s employees can’t see the data.

TRESORIT

PRICE: 5GB free; plans start at 20GB for $8 a month

PLATFORMS: Windows, Mac OS X, iOS, Android

Tresorit (tresorit.com) is a cloud storage provider that claims to offer “a truly secure cloud storage service.” Security features include client-side encryption, secure data transfer, and secure data centers that are equipped with physical security measures against intrusion as well as uninterruptible power and backup systems.

Like Wuala, Tresorit encrypts your data on your local machine to ensure that your files are protected at all times. It, too, practices a zero-knowledge password policy, which means that nobody in the company can ever access your password or decryption keys. Of course, the drawback of such a policy is that if you forget your password, you’re out of luck (you’ll have to create a new account, and you’ll lose all of your data in the cloud).

Tresorit’s main difference from Wuala, and other mainstream cloud storage services, is that it lets you turn any folder on your device into a secure tresor—German for “vault” or “safe.” What this means is that you do not have to drag and drop files into a special sync folder. Instead, you can simply right-click on an existing folder and “tresor it.” This is especially convenient if you’re digitally organized and you’d prefer not to rearrange your files into one sync folder.

Security features include client-side encryption, secure data transfer, and physically secured data centers.

Tresor offers 5GB of space for free. An additional 20GB will cost you $8 per month.

McAFEE PERSONAL LOCKER

PRICE: 1GB free with a subscription to McAfee LiveSafe ($80 per year)

PLATFORMS: Windows 8, iOS, Android

McAfee’s Personal Locker is a cloud storage vault that you manage via your smartphone or Windows 8 device. It stores up to 1GB of data, which you can access from anywhere-but only after you’ve jumped through a series of security hoops.

The app requires voice recognition, biometric data (facial recognition), and a PIN to verify your identity before giving you access to your files. Every. Single. Time. You can choose to set certain files as low priority (you just have to enter a PIN to access them), but where’s the fun in that?

While it’s definitely not the sort of service you’d want to use for everyday cloud storage, Personal Locker would work well for sensitive documents that you may need to access from anywhere, such as legal documents, medical records, or copies of your passport or birth certificate. Personal Locker is free with a subscription to McAfee LiveSafe (go.pcworld.com/livesafereview), which costs $80 per year (go.pcworld.com/mcafeelivesafe).

The app requires voice recognition, biometric data, and a PIN to verify your identity before giving you access.

ENCRYPT FILES LOCALLY

Depending on your current situation, switching to a different cloud storage provider can be frustrating, time-consuming, and potentially more expensive-especially when providers such as Copy (www.copy.com) offer 15GB of free storage (and sometimes more) right off the bat. If you’d rather not start from scratch, you can still rely on client-side encryption to keep your files safe and secure, while continuing to use your insecure, mainstream cloud storage provider. Applications such as TrueCrypt (donation requested; www.truecrypt.org), for instance, will let you encrypt your files inside your Dropbox folder. You’ll need to install TrueCrypt on any device you want to access the files from.
